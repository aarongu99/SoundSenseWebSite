For caregivers

Peace of mind.

Not surveillance.

Supporting a deaf or hard of hearing family member is about showing up when it matters, not monitoring everything. SoundSense runs on their phone, for their benefit first. The caregiver alert layer, coming in Pro, is opt-in from their side, scoped to emergencies, and designed around their autonomy.

Scenarios

What this looks like.

Elderly parent with late-onset hearing loss.

They are still independent. Groceries, friends, their own home. SoundSense runs on their iPhone and gives them sound awareness during the day. Later, with Pro, you can opt in to receiving critical alerts only, smoke alarm, CO alarm, as a safety net. They approve it, and they can turn it off any time.

Adult sibling living independently.

A deaf sibling lives alone, works, travels. They do not need you to manage their life. They might welcome you being on the list for the once-a-year kind of event, a fire alarm going off in the middle of the night, and would be (rightly) frustrated by an app that pinged you every time their microwave finished.

Shared household awareness.

Multiple people in a family, deaf and hearing, share a house. SoundSense runs on each person's phone. If something critical happens, like a fire alarm in the night, every household SoundSense triggers. Everyone is in the loop, no one is being tracked.

Emergency-only caregiver alerts (Pro).

When Pro launches, the user can whitelist you as an emergency contact. You will receive notifications only for the critical tier, not microwave beeps, not doorbells. They control the list, and they can revoke access at any time.

Who owns what

The user is in charge.

The person wearing the phone is the primary user of SoundSense, and every setting belongs to them. You, as a caregiver, are a secondary audience. That framing is not incidental. It is how the product is designed. We do not let a caregiver see the full timeline without the user's consent. We do not let a caregiver turn features on and off remotely. We do not build surveillance into a deaf-community product.

When Pro launches with caregiver alerts, they will be scoped to the critical tier of sounds (fire alarm, CO alarm, a small set of verified-critical custom sounds), and the user chooses which caregivers receive which events. They can revoke access from any caregiver at any moment, from inside the app. That is the design constraint, not a policy we can change later.

Get started

Three steps.

1. Install on their phone

The primary user is your family member. Help them install SoundSense on their iPhone if they want the help, step back if they don't.

2. Let them configure

What sounds to detect, how urgent each one is, whether to use Apple Watch: these are their decisions. You are there if they ask for input.

3. Wait for Pro

If caregiver alerts are important for your situation, join the Pro waitlist. Tell us your story. It helps us get the feature right.

Caregiver questions

What you might ask.

SoundSense assumes the user can keep a phone charged and nearby. If that is not realistic for your situation, say someone with advanced cognitive decline, a dedicated in-home alert system (smoke detector with a strobe, bed-shaker alarm, flashing doorbell) will be more reliable. SoundSense is not the right tool for every scenario, and we want to be honest about that.
