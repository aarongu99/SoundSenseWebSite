For late-deafened adults

Adjusting to hearing loss

at your own pace.

Losing hearing later in life changes what you notice and how you notice it. SoundSense is a tool for the transition, not a solution to it, and not a drama about it. It runs quietly in the background and surfaces the things you care about.

Scenarios

What changes,

and where SoundSense fits.

The first months.

Right after diagnosis or a sudden change, you are still rebuilding your intuition about what is normal. SoundSense handles the short-term gap: doorbells, alarms, your name, the microwave. It does not ask you to become an expert in it overnight.

Routines that depended on hearing.

Cooking timers, the buzz of the dryer, the doorbell while you are working in the back of the house. SoundSense absorbs the routines you used to handle automatically and gives you visual and haptic replacements that integrate into the same flow.

Feeling safe at home.

A smoke alarm, a window alarm, a fire alarm in a neighboring apartment: the safety sounds you used to hear without thinking. SoundSense catches those on your phone and, with Plus, on your wrist. Your home stays audibly aware even when you are not.

Your own pace.

You do not have to turn everything on day one. Start with two or three sounds you know you miss, add more as you learn what matters. The app does not push you toward a specific configuration.

A day in the life

How it fits.

You wake up. Your alarm woke you, trained on your specific bedside clock, with a strong haptic and, if you wear an Apple Watch, a wrist buzz. Coffee. You glance at the timeline and see nothing unusual overnight.

You are working in the back of the house. The doorbell rings at the front. Your phone, in your pocket, vibrates with the pattern you chose. You knew the sound was there; you did not have to hear it. Later, you put something in the microwave and walk away. It beeps. Your phone gives you a soft heads-up.

At dinner with family, someone says your name from across the table when you are looking at your phone. SoundSense notices; your phone vibrates; you look up. Small, specific, repeatable. That is the point.

Get started

Three gentle steps.

1. Install free

No account required. Grant microphone and notifications. Takes a minute.

2. Turn on two sounds

Start small. Doorbell and smoke alarm is plenty. Add others over weeks, not days.

3. Let it be background

It is a tool, not a project. Keep your phone nearby, do what you already do.

Quick questions

For this transition.

Family sharing is part of SoundSense Pro, which launches after the free app is established. In the meantime, the free app works well for the primary user. If sharing is important, join the Pro waitlist.
SoundSense is a tool, not a therapy. It takes some of the cognitive load off so you can focus on the parts of the transition that are about relationships, work, and identity. Audiologists, support groups, and communities of late-deafened adults are where the deeper work happens.
The app icon is standard iOS; you can hide it in App Library or a folder if you prefer. The alerts themselves can be visual-only and silent. No one nearby has to know SoundSense is running unless you tell them.
